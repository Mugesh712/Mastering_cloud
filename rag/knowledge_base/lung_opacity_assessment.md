# Lung Opacity — Differential Diagnosis and Assessment

## Source: Fleischner Society Glossary of Terms, 2024 / ACR Appropriateness Criteria

### Radiological Interpretation of Lung Opacity
- Lung opacity refers to any area of increased density on a chest radiograph that obscures normal lung markings.
- Differential includes: pneumonia, atelectasis, pleural effusion, pulmonary edema, hemorrhage, or neoplasm.
- Focal opacity: localized to one lobe or segment — suggests pneumonia, mass, or localized atelectasis.
- Diffuse opacity: involves multiple lobes bilaterally — suggests pulmonary edema, ARDS, or diffuse infection.
- Ground-glass opacity (GGO): hazy opacification without obscuring underlying structures — suggests partial airspace filling or interstitial process.
- Consolidation: dense opacification with air bronchograms — suggests complete airspace filling (pneumonia, hemorrhage).
- Reticular opacity: network-like pattern — suggests interstitial lung disease or fibrosis.
[Source: Fleischner Society Glossary of Terms for Thoracic Imaging, 2024 Edition]

### Clinical Correlation Protocol
- Clinical symptoms MUST be correlated with imaging findings before diagnosis.
- Fever + productive cough + focal opacity = high probability of bacterial pneumonia.
- Dyspnea + bilateral opacities + cardiomegaly = consider pulmonary edema or heart failure.
- Weight loss + focal mass-like opacity + smoking history = urgent malignancy workup required.
- Acute onset dyspnea + unilateral opacity + pleuritic chest pain = consider pulmonary embolism with infarction.
- History of trauma + opacity = consider pulmonary contusion or hemothorax.
[Source: ACR Appropriateness Criteria for Acute Respiratory Illness, 2024]

### Recommended Imaging Workup
- Initial: PA and lateral chest radiograph (standard two-view).
- If opacity persists on follow-up X-ray at 4-6 weeks: high-resolution CT (HRCT) of the chest.
- CT characteristics to evaluate: margins (smooth vs spiculated), enhancement pattern, cavitation, lymphadenopathy.
- If mass suspected: CT-guided biopsy or bronchoscopy with BAL (bronchoalveolar lavage).
- PET-CT for metabolic characterization if CT findings are indeterminate and malignancy is in the differential.
[Source: British Thoracic Society Guidelines for Investigation of Unilateral Pleural Disease, 2024]

### Treatment Based on Etiology
- Infectious (pneumonia): empirical antibiotics — Amoxicillin-Clavulanate 625mg TID for 7-10 days, or IV Ceftriaxone 1g daily + Azithromycin 500mg daily for hospitalized patients.
- Pulmonary edema: IV Furosemide 40-80mg, fluid restriction, oxygen therapy, treat underlying cardiac cause.
- Atelectasis: incentive spirometry, chest physiotherapy, mobilization, bronchoscopy if obstructive cause suspected.
- Malignancy: multidisciplinary tumor board referral, staging workup, treatment per oncology protocol.
- Pleural effusion: diagnostic thoracentesis (send for protein, LDH, glucose, cell count, cytology, cultures).
[Source: Harrison's Principles of Internal Medicine, 22nd Edition, Chapter 289]

### Follow-up and Monitoring
- Follow-up chest X-ray in 4-6 weeks to document resolution or persistence.
- If opacity resolves completely: no further imaging needed, routine follow-up.
- If opacity persists or enlarges: HRCT within 2 weeks, pulmonology referral.
- Pulmonary function tests (PFT) for patients with persistent dyspnea or suspected restrictive pattern.
- Pulmonologist appointment within 1 week for unresolved opacities.
- Emergency signs: worsening breathlessness, hemoptysis (coughing blood), high fever > 39C, chest pain at rest.
- 3-month follow-up imaging to confirm complete resolution for previously abnormal findings.
[Source: ACR Follow-up Recommendations for Incidental Pulmonary Findings, 2024]

### Prognosis
- Infectious causes: most resolve completely within 4-8 weeks with appropriate antibiotic therapy.
- Atelectasis: typically resolves with respiratory therapy and mobilization within 1-2 weeks.
- Early detection of malignant opacity: 5-year survival varies from 15-70% depending on stage at diagnosis.
- Full recovery expected for most benign causes with appropriate treatment.
- Regular monitoring ensures optimal outcomes and prevents complications like abscess formation or empyema.
[Source: European Respiratory Journal, Pulmonary Opacity Outcomes Review 2024]
