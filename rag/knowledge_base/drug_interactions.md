# Drug Interactions and Contraindications — Respiratory Therapeutics

## Source: FDA Drug Safety Communications / UpToDate Pharmacology Database, 2025

### Remdesivir (Veklury) — COVID-19
- Contraindicated in patients with eGFR < 30 mL/min (contains sulfobutylether-beta-cyclodextrin which accumulates in renal impairment).
- Hepatotoxicity risk: monitor ALT/AST before and during treatment. Discontinue if ALT > 10x upper limit of normal.
- Drug interaction: Chloroquine and Hydroxychloroquine may reduce antiviral activity of Remdesivir — avoid co-administration.
- No significant interaction with Dexamethasone — safe to use together (WHO-recommended combination for severe COVID).
- Pregnancy: limited data, use only if benefit outweighs risk. Not recommended during breastfeeding.
[Source: FDA Veklury Prescribing Information, Revised 2025]

### Nirmatrelvir/Ritonavir (Paxlovid) — COVID-19
- CRITICAL: Ritonavir is a potent CYP3A4 inhibitor — interacts with MANY medications.
- Contraindicated with: statins (simvastatin, lovastatin), certain anticoagulants (rivaroxaban), immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, cyclosporine at standard doses), sedatives (midazolam, triazolam).
- Grapefruit and grapefruit juice: AVOID — inhibits CYP3A4 and potentiates Ritonavir effects, increasing toxicity risk.
- Renal dosing: reduce to 150mg/100mg twice daily if eGFR 30-59 mL/min. Contraindicated if eGFR < 30 mL/min.
- Must be started within 5 days of symptom onset for efficacy. No benefit shown after Day 5.
[Source: FDA Paxlovid EUA Fact Sheet, 2025 / UpToDate Drug Interactions Database]

### Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) — Influenza Pneumonia
- Generally well-tolerated with few significant drug interactions.
- Mild interactions: may reduce efficacy of live influenza vaccine (LAIV) — avoid LAIV for 2 weeks after Oseltamivir.
- Renal dosing: reduce to 75mg once daily if CrCl 30-60 mL/min. 30mg once daily if CrCl 10-30 mL/min.
- GI side effects (nausea, vomiting) in 10-15% of patients — take with food to reduce.
- Safe in pregnancy (Category C) — benefits generally outweigh risks in confirmed influenza.
[Source: WHO Model List of Essential Medicines, Oseltamivir Monograph 2025]

### Dexamethasone — Anti-inflammatory (COVID/Severe Pneumonia)
- Recommended ONLY for patients requiring oxygen supplementation. NOT for mild cases without hypoxia — associated with worse outcomes in non-hypoxic patients.
- Increases blood glucose: monitor closely in diabetic patients. May require insulin dose adjustment.
- Immunosuppressive: increases risk of secondary infections (bacterial, fungal). Watch for new fever or clinical deterioration.
- Drug interactions: reduces efficacy of oral anticoagulants (warfarin) — monitor INR closely. Enhances hypokalemia risk with diuretics.
- Taper recommendation: 6mg daily for 10 days, then stop. For courses > 10 days, taper over 3-5 days to avoid adrenal insufficiency.
[Source: RECOVERY Trial Protocol, Dexamethasone Arm — Oxford University / WHO Recommendation]

### Antibiotic Interactions in Pneumonia Treatment
- Azithromycin: QT prolongation risk — avoid with other QT-prolonging drugs (fluoroquinolones, antiarrhythmics, certain antipsychotics). ECG monitoring recommended in patients > 65 years.
- Amoxicillin-Clavulanate: hepatotoxicity risk (cholestatic, typically 1-6 weeks after completion). Avoid in patients with prior amoxicillin-related liver injury.
- Ceftriaxone: incompatible with calcium-containing IV solutions — do NOT mix. Can cause biliary sludging — monitor for right upper quadrant pain.
- Fluoroquinolones (Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin): FDA Black Box Warning — tendon rupture, peripheral neuropathy, CNS effects. Use only when no alternative available.
[Source: FDA Drug Safety Communications, Antibiotic Warnings Database 2025]

### Common Supplement Interactions
- Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol): safe at doses up to 4000 IU/day. High-dose therapy (60,000 IU/week) requires monitoring of serum calcium — hypercalcemia risk.
- Zinc supplements: doses > 40mg/day can cause copper deficiency with prolonged use (> 4 weeks). Limit to 20-50mg daily for 2-week courses during acute illness.
- Vitamin C: doses > 2000mg/day may cause GI distress and increase oxalate kidney stone risk. Recommended: 500-1000mg daily during infection.
- Iron supplements: reduce absorption of fluoroquinolones and tetracyclines — separate dosing by 2 hours minimum.
- NAC (N-Acetylcysteine): mucolytic, generally safe. May potentiate anticoagulant effects — monitor if on blood thinners.
[Source: NIH Office of Dietary Supplements, Fact Sheets 2025]
